Introduce the subject with the help of a metaphor (15 min @ 15)

The problem is that of renovating an entire apartment building.

Sequential computing is like doing it yourself.

Parallel computing is like doing it with your friends.

Perfectly parallel would the problem be if each friend renovated a different apartment building. Since everyone works on a different building there would be no need to synchronize or communicate.

A more interesting parallel problem is when you only renovate one building and therefore have to coordinate your work. The more friends, the more challenging the coordination becomes. Ten friends are much easier to keep track of than 100 or 1000.

To divide the work between the group of friends, it is necessary to decompose the problem into distinct tasks, each performed by one person. This is called task decomposition.

A task could be big as in renovate apartment 101 or small as in paint the bathroom ceiling in apartment 101. This is the task granularity.

The more tasks the more parallelism.

But the smaller the tasks the less efficient they will be to execute. This is a very important point that makes parallel programming more complicated. Compare moving one bucket of paint across a room two times (two small tasks) versus moving two buckets of paint at once (one big task). While you move a bit slower carrying two buckets, you probably move the paint faster this way, meaning that the bigger task is more efficient to execute.

Hence, we don't want too few tasks since then we don't get enough work to keep all friends busy. But at the same time we also don't want too many tasks since they will then become inefficient to execute, slowing the whole process down even though everyone appears to be busy.

The "right" task granularity (size of the tasks) is therefore related to the number of friends. Few friends, big tasks. Many friends, small tasks.

Tasks often cannot be performed in any order you like. Tasks are dependent on each other. One task must finish before another can start. You can't paint the wall before you have moved the furniture. This is a precedence constraint. Finding the set of dependencies is called dependence analysis.

The tasks and their dependencies can be expressed by a graph. A directed acyclic graph (DAG), to be precise. The nodes are the task. The edges are the dependencies. Edge from A to B means A must finish before B starts. This is a task graph.

With our task graph in hand, we must decide who does what and when. This is scheduling. The schedule (list of who does what and when) must respect the dependencies expressed by the task graph.

There are shared resources such as elevators. Access to the elevator needs to be synchronized causing idle friends waiting for the elevator and doing nothing productive. This is one source of parallel overhead and one of the reasons why you will not go 10 times faster just by being 10 friends sharing the work.

There is communication (not the verbal kind) when the one who takes the furniture down to ground level hands it over to the one driving the truck to the furniture repair shop. Communication takes time and is another source of parallel overhead.

Describe the examination (10 min @ 30)

To pass the course you need to complete:

  • 5 labs (3 programming, 1 experimentation/writing, 1 reading/writing)
  • 1 exam (4 hours)

If you fail an exam then there are two more chances scheduled this year.

If you fail a lab, then there is another deadline shortly after. If you fail that one too, then there is a final chance in August.

If you fail the course then you have to retake the course next year. Completed assignments/exams may or may not carry over (assume that they are not). It is up to the teacher next year to determine on a case by case basis.
